The Architecture of Long-Term Potentiation

At the heart of our ability to learn and remember lies a persistent strengthening of synapses based on recent patterns of activity. These are patterns that create a physiological bridge between the ephemeral and the permanent.

The Hebbian Postulate

#1 When an axon of cell A is near enough to excite a cell B and repeatedly or persistently takes part in firing it, some growth process or metabolic change takes place in one or both cells such that A's efficiency, as one of the cells firing B, is increased.

"Neurons that fire together, wire together." — Carla Shatz

Long-term potentiation (LTP) is considered one of the major cellular mechanisms that underlies learning and memory. In the hippocampus, LTP is characterized by a long-lasting increase in synaptic signaling.
